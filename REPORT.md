# Secondary Raw Materials in Norwegian Industry
## EiT 2026 -- Sirk Norge / NTNU Experts in Teams

**Data collected:** February 2026
**Report generated:** March 2026
**Dataset:** 109 Norwegian companies across 22 NACE codes

---

## Executive Summary

This report analyses the use of Secondary Raw Materials (SRM) across 109 Norwegian industrial companies, spanning metals, plastics, construction, chemicals, waste management, and energy sectors. The dataset covers companies with a combined revenue of approximately NOK 218 billion and roughly 27,000 employees.

**Key headline findings:**

- **60% of companies** (66 of 109) show at least some evidence of SRM usage
- Only **17 companies (15.6%)** use SRM as a core feedstock (Tier 3)
- **43 companies (39.4%)** have no documented SRM use whatsoever
- The **top 5 SRM flows by volume** exceed 3.5 million tonnes per year combined
- **Recycled plastics** (A4) is the most widespread SRM category (43 input records across ~30 companies), but usage is mostly small-scale
- **Recycled metals** (A3) has the highest share of core-level (T3) usage, reflecting the metals industry's structural reliance on scrap
- Two SRM categories -- **A8 (Rubber & Tyres)** and **B2 (Biomass Fuels)** -- have zero documented usage across all 109 companies

## 1. Tier Distribution

Companies were classified into four tiers based on the significance of their SRM usage:

| Tier | Meaning | Count | Share |
|------|---------|-------|-------|
| **T3** | Core / significant SRM use | 17 | 15.6% |
| **T2** | Meaningful / established use | 32 | 29.4% |
| **T1** | Minor / pilot use | 17 | 15.6% |
| **T0** | No evidence found | 43 | 39.4% |

The distribution reveals a polarised landscape: roughly 45% of companies have meaningful-or-better SRM integration (T2+T3), while nearly 40% show no evidence at all. The T1 group (15.6%) represents companies with pilot projects or product-level recycled content claims that have not yet scaled.

### 2.2 Key Observations by Category

**A4 -- Reprocessed Plastics: Breadth without depth.** The most common category by far, but dominated by T1/T2 ratings. Most plastics companies integrate small shares of recycled content into specific products (e.g., 30% recycled PE in packaging film) rather than making recycled plastic a core feedstock. Only Norfolier GreenTec (26,000 t/yr) operates at substantial scale.

**A3 -- Metals Scrap: The structural recyclers.** Nearly half of all metals scrap inputs (10 of 21) are rated T3. This reflects the physical reality that electric arc furnace steelmaking and secondary aluminium smelting are inherently scrap-based processes. The aluminium recycling ecosystem (Hydro, Speira, Alcoa, Metallco) and steel recycling (7 Steel Nordic) form the backbone of Norwegian SRM usage.

**B1 -- Waste-to-Energy: 100% T3.** All three waste-to-energy operators (Hafslund Celsio, BIR Ressurs, Returkraft) are T3 by definition -- their entire business model is built on processing residual waste as fuel.

**A8 and B2 -- Missing categories.** No companies in the dataset use recycled rubber/tyres or biomass fuels as SRM inputs. This may represent a genuine gap in the Norwegian industrial landscape or a data collection blind spot.

### 3.2 Aggregate SRM Volumes

The **combined quantified SRM volume** across all companies exceeds **5 million tonnes per year**. The five largest flows account for over 90% of total quantified volume:

1. **Chemical byproducts** (Yara): 1.93 Mt -- representing 21.5% of Yara's raw material inflows
2. **Organic bio-residues** (St1 Biokraft): 0.91 Mt -- fish silage, food waste, sludge for biogas
3. **Steel scrap** (7 Steel Nordic): 0.70 Mt -- 100% EAF steelmaking at Mo i Rana
4. **Aluminium scrap** (Hydro): 0.45 Mt -- post-consumer scrap across multiple plants
5. **Residual waste** (WtE combined): 0.71 Mt -- Hafslund Celsio + BIR + Returkraft

### 3.3 Data Quality Concerns

- **67% of inputs** have "medium" confidence ratings
- Many "high confidence" figures are actually **permit limits**, not confirmed actual throughput (e.g., Speira, Norfolier GreenTec, Ferroglobe)
- **Group vs. entity attribution** is a persistent issue: Yara's 1.93 Mt figure covers the entire international group, not just Norwegian operations
- Only 27% of SRM inputs have any quantification at all

## 4. Sector Deep-Dives

### 4.1 Aluminium -- Norway's Secondary Metals Powerhouse

Norway hosts one of Europe's most developed aluminium recycling ecosystems:

| Company | Role | Key SRM Volume | Products |
|---------|------|----------------|----------|
| **Norsk Hydro ASA** | Primary + recycling | 451,000 t/yr post-consumer scrap | CIRCAL (75% PCR), CIRCAL 100R (100% PCR) |
| **Speira AS** | Rolling + recycling | 70,000 t dross + 50,000 t salt slag; +22,000 t scrap (planned) | Recycled rolling ingots |
| **Hydro Aluminium AS** | Smelting + recycling | 33,000 t/yr at Hoyanger | Primary + secondary aluminium |
| **Alcoa Norway AS** | Smelting + recycling | 20,000 t/yr at Mosjoen | EcoDura (50%+ recycled) |
| **Metallco Aluminium AS** | Pure recycler | Not quantified | Secondary aluminium |

**Hydro's ambition:** Scale from 451,000 t/yr to **850,000--1,200,000 t/yr by 2030**, which would make it one of the largest aluminium recyclers globally.

**Energy advantage:** Secondary aluminium production uses only **5% of the energy** required for primary production. Combined with Norway's renewable hydropower, this creates a significant competitive advantage for Norwegian recyclers.

### 4.2 Steel -- Electric Arc Furnace Dominance

**7 Steel Nordic Manufacturing AS** (formerly Celsa Armeringsstaal) at Mo i Rana is the centrepiece of Norwegian steel recycling:

- **700,000--770,000 tonnes/year** of steel scrap processed
- 100% electric arc furnace technology powered by renewable hydropower
- Production capacity of 1 million tonnes in steelworks
- Second-largest steel recycler in the Nordics
- Produces reinforcing steel for the construction sector

This single facility represents the largest single-site SRM consumer in Norway by volume (excluding Yara's group-level figure).

### 4.3 Waste-to-Energy -- A Mature Sector

Three WtE operators process a combined **~710,000 tonnes/year** of residual waste:

| Company | Location | Actual Throughput | Permit Limit | Output |
|---------|----------|-------------------|--------------|--------|
| Hafslund Celsio | Oslo (Klemetsrud + Haraldrud) | 377,634 t (2024) | 410,000 t/yr | 1.9 TWh district heating |
| BIR Ressurs | Bergen | ~200,000 t | -- | District heating + electricity |
| Returkraft | Kristiansand | ~130,000 t | 160,000 t/yr | District heating + electricity |

**Hafslund Celsio** is also implementing **Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)** at Klemetsrud, which would make it one of the world's first WtE plants with full carbon capture. Approximately 50% of the CO2 is biogenic.

### 4.4 Construction Materials -- Untapped Potential

The construction sector shows significant SRM potential but relatively low current adoption:

- **Veidekke Industri AS** pioneered 100% reclaimed asphalt pavement (RAP) using parallel drum technology, but disclosed volumes are small (~5,000 t)
- **Heidelberg Materials** (cement/concrete) has 45 environmental permits but the parent entity scored T0; subsidiary-level alternative fuel and recycled aggregate usage is not attributed to the assessed entity
- **Norway's construction waste recycling rate is estimated at only 10--20%**, suggesting enormous room for improvement
- National targets include **50% GHG reduction in road construction by 2030** under the N200 framework

### 4.5 Zinc and Non-Ferrous Metals

- **Boliden Odda AS** produces 35,000--40,000 tonnes of pure zinc annually from secondary materials, representing ~20% of total zinc production
- **Glencore Nikkelverk AS** in Kristiansand (world's largest nickel refinery in the western world) processes recycled metal-bearing feed; approximately one-third of third-party suppliers deliver 100% recycled material
- **Everzinc Norway AS** operates in the secondary zinc space but with limited quantified data

### 4.6 Plastics -- Widespread but Shallow

The plastics sector has the most companies with SRM inputs (A4 category: 43 records) but most usage is product-level:

- Most plastics companies integrate 10--30% recycled content into specific product lines
- **Norfolier GreenTec** is the standout with 26,000 t/yr of recycled PE film
- **BEWI** operates a closed-loop EPS recycling facility in Fredrikstad
- **Norsk Gjenvinning** handles 25,000 t/yr of plastic waste for sorting and recycling
- Many companies cite recycled content but cannot provide annual inbound SRM tonnages

### 4.7 Glass -- A Circular Economy Success Story

**Glasopor AS** stands out as a near-perfect circular economy example:

- Product (foam glass insulation) is made from **100% recycled glass packaging**
- Sources: sorted glass cullets from Norwegian household collection, glass fines, recovered glass wool waste
- **55,000 tonnes** of recycled glass received at the Onsoy facility (2022)
- Applying for increased production at Fredrikstad
- Demonstrates that T3-level circularity is achievable even for smaller companies (NOK 138M revenue)

### 4.8 Bio-Based Materials

- **Forestia AS** uses "large amounts" of sawmill by-product wood chips as core chipboard feedstock (T3), with a NOK 80 million investment project to incorporate demolition timber -- addressing Norway's 1 million tonnes/year of demolition wood waste
- **St1 Biokraft AS** processes 914,906 t/yr of organic residues (fish silage, food waste, sludge) for biogas production at Skogn, making it the second-largest quantified SRM flow in the dataset

## 8. Circular Economy Loops Identified

The data reveals several functioning or emerging circular value chains in Norway:

### 8.1 Steel Loop
Post-consumer steel scrap --> Collection --> 7 Steel Nordic (Mo i Rana, EAF) --> Reinforcing steel --> Construction --> Demolition --> Scrap collection (repeat)

### 8.2 Aluminium Loop
Used aluminium (cans, profiles, vehicles) --> Collection --> Hydro/Speira/Alcoa recycling facilities --> CIRCAL/EcoDura products --> Consumer products --> End-of-life collection (repeat)

### 8.3 Glass Loop
Household glass packaging --> Municipal collection (returpunkt) --> Glasopor (Onsoy) --> Foam glass insulation --> Construction (permanent incorporation)

### 8.4 EPS/Plastics Loop
Used EPS packaging --> Collection --> BEWI Fredrikstad --> Compression, extrusion, regranulation --> New EPS products (closed loop)

### 8.5 Wood Loop (Emerging)
Demolition timber (~1M tonnes/year nationally) --> Cleaning & sorting --> Forestia chipboard production --> Building panels --> (End of life --> repeat)

### 8.6 Organic Waste Loop
Fish silage + food waste + sludge --> St1 Biokraft / BIR Ressurs --> Biogas/biomethane (energy) + Digestate (fertiliser) --> Agriculture --> Food production (repeat)

### 8.7 Waste-to-Energy Loop (with CCS)
Non-recyclable residual waste --> Hafslund Celsio (Klemetsrud) --> District heating (1.9 TWh) + Electricity + CO2 capture --> Permanent storage (Longship/Northern Lights CCS project)

## 9. Data Gaps and Limitations

### 9.1 Quantification Gap
- **73% of SRM inputs lack quantified volumes** -- the evidence is qualitative only (product pages, sustainability statements, press releases)
- Where quantities exist, **67% are "medium" confidence**, relying on press releases, permit limits, or EPD process descriptions rather than audited sustainability reports

### 9.2 Entity vs. Group Attribution
- Many SRM disclosures are at the **parent/group level** (e.g., Yara International, Hydro, BEWI, Berry) rather than the specific Norwegian legal entity assessed
- This inflates apparent SRM volumes for some companies (Yara's 1.93 Mt is group-wide) while understating others (Heidelberg Materials Norway, the holding company, shows T0 despite major subsidiary operations)

### 9.3 Missing Sectors
- **Agriculture** (NACE 01.11) companies in the dataset are mostly social institutions, not large-scale farms that would use digestate
- **Sawmilling** (NACE 16.1) returned zero companies from the business registry
- **Electricity production** (NACE 35.11) missed major producers like Statkraft, likely due to NACE classification differences

### 9.4 Unreported Use
- A significant number of T0 companies may have unreported SRM use -- the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, particularly for companies without public sustainability reports
- Construction companies in particular may use recycled aggregates or reclaimed asphalt without documenting it in public sources

## 10. Key Takeaways and Recommendations

### 10.1 What's Working
1. **Metals recycling** is mature and commercially viable in Norway, with aluminium and steel scrap forming core feedstocks for major producers
2. **Waste-to-energy** is fully established, with CCS adding a new dimension
3. **Specific circular economy success stories** (Glasopor, Forestia, BEWI) demonstrate that 100% recycled feedstock models are commercially viable
4. **Regulatory tailwinds** (CSRD, N200, CCS incentives) are pushing companies toward greater transparency and SRM adoption

### 10.2 Where the Gaps Are
1. **Plastics recycling** is widespread but shallow -- most companies have product-level claims without significant inbound SRM volumes
2. **Construction materials** recycling lags far behind potential (10--20% construction waste recycling rate)
3. **Rubber/tyres (A8) and biomass fuels (B2)** are completely absent from the dataset
4. **Data quality** is a major challenge -- most companies cannot or do not report entity-level SRM tonnages
5. **39.4% of companies** show no SRM engagement at all, including several major industrial firms

### 10.3 Opportunities
1. **Aluminium scale-up**: Hydro's plan to grow from 451,000 t to 850,000--1,200,000 t by 2030 could make Norway a European leader in secondary aluminium
2. **Demolition wood**: Forestia's project to process Norway's 1 million t/yr of demolition timber represents a significant untapped resource
3. **Construction sector**: With only 10--20% recycling rates, the construction sector has the largest potential for SRM growth
4. **Industrial symbiosis**: Cross-company material flows (e.g., Eramet's FeMn slag from Sauda to SiMn production at Kvinesdal) could be expanded and formalised
5. **Biogas expansion**: The emerging biogas sector (St1 Biokraft, BIR Ressurs, Den Magiske Fabrikken) has growth potential for both energy and fertiliser production

## 1. The Most Surprising Numbers

### St1 Biokraft: 16 Employees, 915,000 Tonnes
The single most jaw-dropping statistic in the entire dataset. **St1 Biokraft AS has just 16 employees but processes 914,906 tonnes of organic waste annually** -- that is **57,182 tonnes per employee per year.** Their primary feedstock is 70,000 tonnes of fish silage from the Norwegian salmon farming industry, fed into an anaerobic digestion biogas plant at Skogn, alongside process water from Norske Skog's pulp and paper mill. This is industrial symbiosis at its most efficient.

And their emissions math is wild: St1 Biokraft claims **more than 100% emissions reduction** when using manure feedstock. This sounds impossible but makes sense -- the biogas not only replaces fossil fuel, but it also *prevents the methane emissions that manure would otherwise release*, creating a net-negative carbon accounting.

### 7 Steel Nordic: 770,000 Tonnes of Scrap Above the Arctic Circle
At Mo i Rana, above the Arctic Circle, **7 Steel Nordic Manufacturing** (formerly Celsa Armeringsstaal) recycles over **770,000 tonnes of steel scrap annually** using electric arc furnaces powered by renewable hydropower. That is roughly equivalent to the weight of **110 Eiffel Towers** recycled every year. Their EPD reveals that 62% of scrap is sourced domestically from Norway, with 38% imported. Statkraft signed a deal securing **2.4 TWh of renewable power over 9 years** to keep the furnaces running green.

### Yara: The Hidden SRM Giant
Yara Norge AS, known as a fertilizer producer, quietly procures **1,933,000 tonnes of byproduct and waste-stream chemical raw materials** from suppliers annually -- by far the largest SRM flow in the dataset. This represents **21.5% of their total raw material inflows**. Examples include sulfur from crude oil desulfurization and ammonium sulphate from synthetic fibre production. As a footnote, they also switched all product bags in Europe to contain 30% recycled plastic (4,427 tonnes). The contrast between the 1.93 million tonne chemical SRM and the 4,427 tonne plastic SRM is itself remarkable.

### GE Healthcare: The Pharmaceutical Surprise
A completely unexpected entry: **GE Healthcare AS** (NOK 13.7 billion revenue) operates a biological treatment plant producing **5 million Nm3/year of biogas** and burns **30,000 tonnes/year of recovered hazardous-waste solvents** as boiler fuel. A pharmaceutical/medical devices company with serious circular energy infrastructure -- not the sector you would expect to find managing industrial by-products at this scale.

### Pelagia: 180,000 Tonnes of Fish By-Products
**Pelagia AS**, a seafood processing company, converts **180,000 tonnes/year of fish by-products and trimmings** into fish meal, fish oil, and other products (high confidence data). This makes Pelagia the largest marine circular economy player in the dataset, turning what would be waste from filleting operations into valuable animal feed ingredients.

### The 95% Energy Saving
Speira's data shows that recycling aluminium requires only **5% of the energy** needed for primary production. For every tonne of recycled aluminium, Norway avoids consuming the energy equivalent needed to smelt 19 additional tonnes from raw bauxite. Combined with Norway's renewable hydropower, this makes Norwegian secondary aluminium among the lowest-carbon metal on earth.

## 2. Hidden Gems: Small Companies Doing Remarkable Things

### Rolvsoy Metallindustri: The 5-Person T3 Champion
With just **5 employees and NOK 8.6 million in revenue**, Rolvsoy Metallindustri AS is the smallest company in the entire 158-company dataset to achieve the highest tier (T3). Their entire aluminium casting operation runs on recycled scrap. Revenue per employee is just NOK 1.7 million, suggesting a small artisanal operation that is nonetheless fully circular.

### Oldroyd: Turning Ocean Trash into Tunnel Infrastructure
Oldroyd (T2) manufactures concrete spacers from **100% recycled sea plastic** -- a mixture of polypropylene and polyethylene fished from the ocean. Each tunnel project requires 300,000--400,000 spacers at 150 grams each, meaning **45--60 tonnes of ocean plastic per tunnel.** They also use 10% recycled plastic in tunnel waterproofing membranes, with potential scale of 500+ tonnes per project.

### Steen-Hansen: Recycled Refrigerators Protecting Salmon Farms
Steen-Hansen (T2, just 27 employees, NOK 361 million revenue) uses copper oxide derived from *"resirkulerte kobberror, ledninger, kjoleskeap etc."* -- recycled copper pipes, wires, and refrigerators -- as the active antifouling component in coatings that protect aquaculture nets. Old household appliances become the chemical shield protecting Norway's massive salmon farming industry. At NOK 13.4 million revenue per employee, they are also one of the most productive companies per headcount in the dataset.

### Glasopor: Every Norwegian Wine Bottle Becomes Insulation
Glasopor (T3, NOK 138 million) converts **100% recycled glass packaging** collected from Norwegian household sorting into foam glass -- a lightweight insulation material for roads and foundations. Their Onsoy facility received **55,000 tonnes of glass in 2022.** The production process crushes recycled glass to fine powder and heat-processes it. Every wine bottle, jam jar, and beer bottle that Norwegians sort ends up as building insulation.

### Jotul: 170+ Years of Casting with Scrap
Jotul AS, the iconic Norwegian fireplace manufacturer founded in 1853, uses **100% recycled scrap iron** and receives deliveries from local scrap dealers **every day.** Their annual report states: *"All cast iron production is with recycled iron, and the foundry is run on hydroelectricity."* This is a heritage brand that has practiced circular economy principles for decades -- likely long before the term existed.

### Ringnes: Your Beer is More Circular Than You Think
**Ringnes AS** (the brewery) is a new T3 entry with **three separate SRM streams**: rPET in plastic bottles, recycled aluminium in beer cans, and a pilot programme for rPET beer kegs. A consumer-facing brand achieving T3 through packaging circularity across multiple material types.

## 3. The Aquaculture Circular Economy -- A Norwegian Innovation

The expanded dataset reveals an even richer web connecting SRM to Norwegian aquaculture -- perhaps the most distinctly Norwegian finding in the data:

1. **St1 Biokraft** processes **70,000 t/year of fish silage from salmon farming** into biogas
2. **Pelagia AS** converts **180,000 t/year of fish by-products** into fish meal and oil
3. **Steen-Hansen** uses **recycled copper from old refrigerators** in aquaculture net antifouling coatings
4. **AKVA Group** is testing the **first circular farming nets from recycled nylon** and first recycled HDPE pens from discarded pens
5. **Plasto** makes walkways from **100% recycled discarded aquaculture pens** (*"gangbaner produsert av 100% gjenvunnet material fra kasserte oppdrettsmerder"*)
6. **BEWI Packaging** markets recycled-feedstock EPS fish boxes (GreenLine) with 60% lower CO2 footprint
7. **Oldroyd** uses **recycled sea plastic** in construction products
8. **SalMar AS** (NOK 23.7 billion) uses marine offcuts in fish feed
9. **Statkraft Energi AS** uses fish-waste-based soft feed at its fish farming operations

This forms a growing circular ecosystem where salmon-farm waste becomes biogas feedstock, fish trimmings become animal feed, old nets become new nets, old pens become walkways, and ocean debris becomes tunnel components. Given that Norway is the world's largest salmon farming nation, this aquaculture-SRM nexus may be a uniquely Norwegian contribution to circular economy thinking.

## 5. Contradictions and Greenwashing Signals

### "Recyclable" Does Not Mean "Recycled"
**Nesseplast** (T0, NOK 225 million) manufactures EPS packaging and proudly states: *"All our products are recyclable and reusable."* Yet their evidence simultaneously reveals: *"The raw material, EPS is based on oil."* They source virgin EPS from BASF with zero recycled content. This is a textbook case of downstream recyclability claims masking upstream virgin-material dependence.

### Polarplast's Circular Language Illusion
**Polarplast** (T0, NOK 110 million) states: *"Alle vaare plastkomponenter er 100% resirkulerbare. Alt av overskuddsmateriell fra vaar produksjon leveres til gjenvinning."* Translation: products are 100% recyclABLE and production surplus is sent for recycling -- but this carefully sidesteps the question of whether they use any recycled *inputs*. They do not.

### MANODA: Refreshingly Honest
By contrast, **MANODA** (T0, NOK 12 million, 10 employees) is perhaps the most candid company in the dataset: *"MANODA bruker kun nytt metall med dokumentert renhetssertifikat"* -- "We use ONLY new/virgin metal with certified purity." This transparency, while showing zero SRM use, is more honest than companies wrapping virgin-material operations in green language.

## 6. The Paradoxes

### Norway's Biggest Recycler is Only Tier 2
**Norsk Gjenvinning** ("Norwegian Recycling") -- the country's largest recycling company with 764 employees, NOK 3.26 billion revenue, and ~2 million tonnes of waste handled annually -- achieves only T2 status. Their website declares: *"Det du kaster, skal ikke til en endestasjon, men til en sirkular ravvarefabrikk"* ("What you throw away shouldn't go to an end station, but to a circular raw material factory"). Yet their own SRM documentation is fragmented across divisions and permits. The irony: the company most associated with recycling in Norway has weaker SRM self-documentation than a 5-person foundry (Rolvsoy) that scores T3.

### 45 Environmental Permits, Zero SRM Evidence
**Heidelberg Materials Norway** has **45 environmental permits** in the Norskeutslipp registry -- more than any other company in the dataset -- yet is rated T0. The explanation: the permits belong to subsidiary operations (cement plants at Brevik and Kjopsvik), while the assessed legal entity is the Oslo-based holding company. Their subsidiary produces Norway's only cement, including the pioneering evoZero net-zero cement with CCS technology -- but none of this is attributable to the specific assessed entity. This is a cautionary tale about entity-level vs. group-level assessment.

### Same NACE Code, Wildly Different Commitment
Among the **14 companies sharing NACE code 22.220** (plastic packaging), tiers span the full range:
- **T0:** 6 companies (43%) -- no SRM evidence at all
- **T1:** 4 companies -- minor/qualitative claims
- **T2:** 3 companies -- meaningful recycled content
- **T3:** 1 company (Norfolier GreenTec) -- 26,000 t/yr of recycled PE film

This proves that SRM adoption is a **strategic choice**, not a sector inevitability. Companies making nearly identical products have completely different material sourcing strategies.

### The Metals Polarization
The metals sector (NACE 24.x) has the most polarised distribution. Companies like Jotul (100% recycled scrap iron, daily deliveries) coexist with Norse Metal Elverum, a similarly-sized foundry (20 employees) rated T0 with zero documented SRM use. Two foundries, roughly the same size, in southeastern Norway, on opposite ends of the circularity spectrum.

### BEWI Group: Internal Contradiction
Within the same corporate group:
- **BEWI Insulation Norge AS** (T2): *"Greenline EPS is made of 100% recycled raw material"*, 3,500 t/yr recycling capacity
- **BEWI Packaging Norway AS** (T1): Only qualitative evidence, no entity-specific SRM volumes

The insulation division has measurable circular practices while the packaging division -- selling products marketed as sustainable -- cannot demonstrate its own SRM intake.

### The Revenue-SRM Disconnect
The expanded dataset makes the disconnect even starker. T3 companies (the most circular) have a combined revenue of just **NOK 36 billion**, while T1 companies (minor/pilot SRM use) have **NOK 238 billion** -- nearly 7x more. The biggest companies by revenue are not the most circular. T0 companies (no SRM evidence) still represent **NOK 106 billion** in combined revenue.

## 7. Circular Economy Loops Worth Highlighting

### The Steel-to-Zinc-to-Steel Loop
**Boliden Odda** processes **Waelz oxide from the steel scrap recycling industry** (since 2008) to recover zinc, producing 35,000--40,000 tonnes of pure zinc annually from secondary materials (~20% of total production). Meanwhile, **7 Steel Nordic** feeds scrap into EAF steelmaking. The zinc coating on galvanized steel scrap becomes a secondary zinc feed for Boliden. This is an **inter-company, inter-metal circular loop operating within Norway** -- one company's waste stream is another's feedstock.

### Offshore Decommissioning: A New Loop
**Aker Solutions AS** receives **85,000 tonnes of decommissioned offshore structures** at its Stord yard for recycling. As North Sea oil platforms reach end-of-life, this creates a new and uniquely Norwegian circular pathway: offshore steel structures built decades ago returning to shore to be recycled into new materials. With Aker Solutions' NOK 51.2 billion revenue and 9,020 employees, this is industrial-scale circular economy.

### Industrial Symbiosis: Eramet's Inter-Site Material Flow
**Eramet Norway** takes ferromanganese slag produced at its **Sauda facility** and ships it to **Kvinesdal** where it serves as raw material for silicomanganese production. The company describes this as *"an important input factor."* Furnace dust and slag are also reintroduced into production processes. This is circular economy within a single company's operations, across two geographic sites.

### The Concrete-to-Concrete Loop (Emerging)
Heidelberg Materials' **ReConcrete technology** recovers sand, gravel, and hardened cement paste from waste concrete. **Olen Betong** replaces 75--100% of natural sand with processed tunnel excavation rock. The RECONC research project is developing recycled construction aggregates with **CO2-binding properties** -- concrete waste that actually *absorbs* CO2 as it is processed.

### The Full Organic Waste Circle
Fish silage + food waste + sludge --> St1 Biokraft / BIR Ressurs --> Biogas/biomethane (energy) + Digestate (fertiliser) --> Agriculture --> Food production --> Organic waste (repeat). BIR's vision statement goes further: *"upcycling food waste into new food for regional utilization."*

### Biogas-to-Plastic: Vartdal Plast's Innovation
**Vartdal Plast** uses waste-derived biogas as EPS raw-material feedstock via mass balance -- an unusual bio-to-plastic pathway where organic waste is converted to biogas, which then replaces fossil gas as a chemical feedstock for making polystyrene. This closes a loop from waste to packaging material.

### Equinor: Oil from Waste Back to Refinery
**Equinor Refining Norway AS** processes **250,000 m3/year of recovered oil** from hazardous waste treatment, plus bio-oils blended into refinery feedstock. Norway's largest oil company feeding secondary materials back into its own refining operations.

## 8. Technology Innovations

### CCS at a Cement Plant -- A World First
Heidelberg Materials' subsidiary operates the Brevik CCS plant -- the **world's first full-scale carbon capture facility at a cement plant.** This produces **evoZero net-zero cement.** Given that cement accounts for ~8% of global CO2 emissions, this positions Norway as the global pioneer in decarbonising one of the hardest-to-abate industrial sectors.

### WAM Foam: Cooler Asphalt, More Recycling
Veidekke's **Warm Asphalt Mix (WAM) Foam technology** reduces asphalt production temperature from ~160C to **115C.** Combined with their **parallel drum technology** enabling production from **100% reclaimed asphalt pavement (RAP)**, this is a double innovation: lower energy AND higher recycled content. Veidekke claims to have built Norway's first plant capable of 100% RAP asphalt.

### Elkem's Sicalo Pilot: Capturing Carbon to Reuse as a Raw Material
Elkem's pilot in Kristiansand captures carbon from process off-gases and **reuses it as a reductant in silicon production.** This is the only example in the entire dataset of carbon capture being treated as an SRM input -- captured CO2 becoming a production feedstock rather than just being stored.

### BRIQSIL: One Company's Waste Enables Another's Circularity
Elkem's BRIQSIL product transforms silicon fines (a waste stream) into briquettes that serve as a ferrosilicon substitute in steelmaking, enabling steelmakers to **use more scrap.** This is a meta-innovation: one company's waste product helps another company increase its own recycled content.

### Kongsberg Maritime: Refurbished Thrusters
**Kongsberg Maritime AS** (NOK 14.8 billion) refurbishes and remanufactures marine thrusters -- extending the life of complex, high-value maritime equipment rather than producing new units. A different kind of SRM: the secondary raw material is the product itself.

## 9. The Svalbard Question: Circularity at the Edge of the World

**Svalbard Energi AS** (T0, NOK 267 million, 30 employees) operates the energy system in **Longyearbyen** -- the world's northernmost settlement with a year-round population. Having recently transitioned away from coal, they now run **diesel power generators and diesel-fired boilers.** Their permit application states: *"primaerforsyningen ved kraftverket er dagens dieselaggregater."*

There is zero SRM use. This is the only company in the dataset located in the Arctic, and it represents a fundamental circularity challenge: **how do you implement secondary raw material use in one of the most remote inhabited locations on Earth?** The logistics of transporting waste feedstock to 78 degrees North make most SRM strategies economically impossible. Svalbard is a reminder that the circular economy achievements documented elsewhere in this dataset are enabled by Norway's mainland infrastructure -- and that extreme locations still face unresolved energy transition challenges.

## 10. The Data Quality Story

### Gold Standard: Hafslund Celsio
Provides the strongest evidence chain in the dataset: exact 2024 tonnage (377,634 tonnes burned), an explicit permit limit (410,000 t/yr), breakdown by waste type, and a clear operational description. High confidence ratings across the board.

### Silver Standard: Ulefos Jernvaerk
Their EPD reveals that scrap iron constitutes **93.23% of their ductile iron product mass** -- one of the highest SRM content percentages in the entire dataset. The declared unit EPD shows **933 kg of scrap iron per 1 tonne of ductile iron product.** This level of granularity is rare.

### The Confidence Gap
- Only **25% of SRM inputs have any quantification at all** -- the rest are qualitative claims
- Many "high confidence" figures are actually **permit limits**, not confirmed throughput
- Group-level vs. entity-level reporting inflates some figures (Yara, Hydro) and obscures others (Heidelberg Materials)

### The Absence-of-Evidence Problem
54 companies are rated T0 -- but for many, this means "no public evidence was found," not "confirmed non-use." Several of these companies may use SRM without documenting it publicly. The construction sector is particularly susceptible: many builders likely use recycled aggregates or reclaimed asphalt routinely without publishing sustainability data. The new T0 companies include **Kongsberg Defence & Aerospace** (NOK 17.1 billion) and **VARD Group** (NOK 10.2 billion) -- major industrial operations where SRM use seems plausible but undocumented.

## 13. The Maturity Hierarchy

The data reveals a clear maturity ranking in Norwegian SRM adoption:

**Mature (SRM is structurally embedded):**
- Metal scrap recycling (steel, aluminium, zinc, iron, nickel)
- Waste-to-energy
- Glass recycling (Glasopor)
- Fish by-product processing (Pelagia)

**Emerging (active but not yet dominant):**
- Asphalt and construction aggregate recycling
- Biogas from organic waste
- Wood by-product utilisation
- Offshore decommissioning (Aker Solutions)
- Consumer packaging circularity (Ringnes, Coca-Cola)

**Early stage (product-level, not system-level):**
- Plastics recycling (widespread claims, limited volumes)
- Chemical feedstock recycling
- Silicon recycling
- Remanufactured industrial components (Kongsberg Maritime, Volvo)

**Emerging from zero (newly represented in dataset):**
- Biomass fuels (B2) -- now 3 entries (Felleskjopet, Optimera, Orkla Foods)
- Biogas as chemical feedstock (Vartdal Plast's mass-balance approach)

**Still absent:**
- Rubber and tyre recycling (A8) -- **zero entries across all 158 companies**

## 14. Norway's Unique Competitive Advantage

A thread running through the entire dataset: **Norway's renewable hydropower transforms the economics of recycling.**

When 7 Steel Nordic melts scrap at Mo i Rana using hydropower, the resulting steel has a dramatically lower carbon footprint than identical EAF steel made with coal-powered electricity in Germany or Poland. When Alcoa melts aluminium scrap at Mosjoen using wind and hydropower, it avoids 4,400 tonnes of CO2 that a gas-powered furnace would emit. Jotul's foundry runs on hydroelectricity. Hydro's CIRCAL aluminium achieves 1.9 kg CO2/kg -- nearly a tenth of the global average.

This creates a compounding advantage: Norwegian recycled metals are not just recycled, they are **recycled with the cleanest energy in Europe.** As carbon border adjustment mechanisms (CBAM) and green procurement standards become more common, this positions Norway's secondary metals sector for a structural competitive advantage that few other countries can replicate.

The expanded dataset reinforces this: major energy and maritime companies (Statkraft, Equinor, Aker Solutions, Kongsberg) are now entering the SRM space, bringing Norway's energy infrastructure advantage to bear on new sectors.

## 15. What the Website Reveals About Methodology

### The Assessment Framework
The project used a custom **13-category MECE taxonomy** (A1-A10 for materials, B1-B3 for energy) and a **5-level tier system** (T0 to T3 plus N/D). Each company was independently assessed based on publicly available evidence, with claims linked to specific sources via numbered citations. The viewer page even uses Chrome's **text fragment highlighting** (`#:~:text=`) to link evidence snippets directly to their source documents.

### The LokiJS Database Approach
The viewer page uses **LokiJS** -- an in-memory JavaScript database -- for client-side querying and sorting. This is an unusual architectural choice for a static site that enables powerful filtering without a backend server. All three pages (dashboard, viewer, analytics) load from a single `aggregated.json` file and share a consistent design system with a dark/light theme toggle persisted to `localStorage`.

### The Quantity Rollup
The analytics page aggregates all quantified SRM inputs by unit and period, revealing the total documented SRM flow across all companies. This is a particularly useful view because it shows not just individual company volumes but the aggregate scale of secondary raw material consumption across Norwegian industry.

## 16. Questions This Data Raises

1. **Why is construction waste recycling so low (10-20%) in a country with such strong metals recycling?** Is it regulatory, economic, or cultural?

2. **What explains the complete absence of rubber/tyre recycling (A8)?** With 158 companies and zero entries, is this a data collection gap or a genuine absence in Norwegian industry?

3. **Could industrial symbiosis be formalised?** Eramet's Sauda-to-Kvinesdal slag flow, the Boliden-7Steel zinc-steel loop, and GE Healthcare's on-site biogas plant happen organically -- what if they were systematically designed?

4. **What is the actual Norwegian share of group-level SRM figures?** Yara's 1.93 Mt, Hydro's 451,000 t, and Speira's 650,000 t are all international figures. The Norway-specific volumes remain unclear.

5. **Why do 54 out of 158 assessed companies (34.2%) show zero SRM evidence?** How many of these are genuine non-users vs. companies that simply do not report?

6. **Can the aquaculture-SRM nexus be expanded?** With Pelagia (180,000 t), St1 Biokraft (70,000 t fish silage), SalMar, Statkraft, and others now in the dataset, the marine circular economy is becoming a major theme. What other aquaculture waste streams could become SRM inputs?

7. **What happens when CSRD/ESRS reporting becomes mandatory for more companies?** Will the T0 count drop significantly as companies are forced to disclose material flows?

8. **How will offshore decommissioning scale?** Aker Solutions' 85,000 t is just the beginning -- hundreds of North Sea platforms will reach end-of-life in the coming decades.

9. **Why are T1 companies (NOK 238 billion combined revenue) not scaling their SRM use?** What barriers prevent pilot programmes from becoming core operations?

10. **Could Svalbard become a circular economy testbed?** The Arctic's remoteness is a barrier, but also an opportunity for innovative closed-loop systems in extreme environments.
